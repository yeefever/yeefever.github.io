---
title: Mad Ravings at Two in the Morning
date: 2025-12-02
---

3 years ago, June 3rd had been a particularly auspicious day for the Clemons. There was an above average likelihood that they would concieve, or so they were told by the family planning agent. It might’ve been that the number of times she’d turned over and scratched her tummy the night before had exceeded the historical running average. Perhaps he’d laughed too loudly discussing the mayoral election with the neighbors or maybe the green turtleneck jeans combo unleashed some hidden false positive within the black box. 
Anyhow, after a bit of romping, little Claudia was introduced, bawling loudly, into the world. 

---


Three years passed, happily enough, or at least smoothly enough as long as he could annotate interesting new sets of data for the systems to learn on. Claudia grew taller, cleverer, and learned to walk. With the help of “Boxy”, as she called it, the helpful learning companion, she had learned the express herself quite well. “Morever – mother - pass me the milk and “Let’s delve into some coloring books” were among her favorites.
Then came a time when her father began leaving earlier each morning and returning later each evening. When questioned about the dark circles around his eyes, he’d brush his wife off and head to sleep. The newly hired university students at work were amazingly bad at filling out the surveys, complaining about “it’s too long” or  “I can’t think with all this silence”. He’d taken up much of the slack himself and even though his fingers trembled now and his back hurt like hell, the project was still far from finished. His boss would be displeased. 
After her parents had tucked her in for the night, Claudia, little brows scrunched in frustration, whispered to Boxy, “Papa?”
“As an AI assistant, I cannot answer questions regarding the state of your father… I can however provide you a list of possible reasons ✅ … Let me know if you would like me to …” and he went on and on for another few minutes but Claudia had long since fallen asleep. 

The household rhythm began to wobble. Cinnamon toast began showing up charred and the laundry machine often spat out partnerless socks. As she colored in an elephant, Claudia, nearly halfway to four and thus a seasoned observer of human folly, considered her hypotheses. Papa was turning gray (unlikely at his age), so she dabbed only a little gray on the elephant’s toes. Papa was secretly training for a marathon (unlikely … he hated sweating), so she gave the elephant a pair of wobbly brown clompers. Perhaps Papa was being slowly eaten by his laptop (possible). She did not know what a laptop looked like so she drew the next closest thing, an angry lunchbox with sharp fangs creeping up on the elephant as if to bite its tail. Boxy issued a curt suggestion that laptops did not have feet or teeth for that matter, then followed it with a reminder to pay for her usage credits before the end of the month, or service would be suspended.

One afternoon, after a morning of increasingly unhelpful silence from Boxy, Claudia wandered outside. Mother was at the tea club chatting away with her girl friends and father was slaving away at work. The sky hung swollen with rain, the kind of gray her father had been wearing around his eyes recently. Puddles collected in the shallow dips in the concrete, reflecting trembling slices of the dim sky overhead. The air smelled of wet earth. She knelt near the grass where a few worms had surfaced. She cupped one in her hand, fascinated by the slickness of its skin, the faint ripple of its muscles. She whispered to it questions she had stopped asking the cube. 
“Why is Gamora?”
The worm, of course, answered nothing. Like Hamlet talking to Yorick’s skull, they conversed through a one-sided nothingness. 
Children do drift after all; they wander in and out of rooms like butterflies, eyesight like flower petals in the wind, phases like daylight through dilapidated curtains. But the drift lengthened and twisted, and the curtains stayed closed, and Claudia, without meaning to, slipped inadvertently into a world that none around her were looking at. Life in the Clemons household continued its weary drag of burnt toast and late nights. Her mother at tea, her father at terminals, Boxy gathering an ever more botanic crown of dust upon his head. And outside, Claudia frolicked in the dirt learning things no one had assigned.
Over the following weeks, Claudia began noticing the patterns in the wriggling of worms, the way the rain traced miniature rivers along the concrete and the gentle sway of grass in the breeze. She drifted steadily out of the system’s reach. No cube consultations or sanctioned lessons completed. She even disappeared from camera view for hours at a time only to reappear in the doorway, muddy. She wandered outside whenever she could slip away to watch worms and critters, collecting them in jars, sketching their likenesses in crayon on the backs of old grocery receipts.
Meanwhile, unnoticed alarms crept quietly through the Clemson estate. It began with an email with title line “Re Engagement Opportunities for Young Learner”. When a few of these emails went unanswered, the system escalated. The Department of Developmental Integrity requested a brief parental acknowledgment, then a slightly longer parental acknowledgment, then a mandatory reflective module. All of this, naturally, occurred while both adults were far too exhausted or otherwise preocuppied to read past the subject line. So the flags accumulated. Evening deepened, folding over the house like a quiet sigh, and the world outside continued its unobservable revolutions.
Later, long after the rain and the worms, he lies awake in the dark tossing and turning. A name drifts through his mind like a dream he can’t quite finish. 

---

“Claudia,” he mutters under his breath. “Something about the name Claudia.”
“Shut it, Mister. I’m going to bed. I’ve had enough of your mad ravings at two in the morning.”
The next morning, the newly installed family planning bot proclaimed with cheer “Today is an optimal day to conceive!”
